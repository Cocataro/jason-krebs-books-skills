# Humanization patterns for Pangram-flagged windows

When a Pangram window comes back `label: "AI-Generated"`, the window text has a craft problem. Pattern-specific fixes below. Tuned to Briar-POV Heartland Fantasy voice; generalize to other POVs as series expands.

These are craft corrections, not obfuscation tactics. Do not chase a score. Address the underlying smoothness.

## Pattern 1 — The "stood as" / "testament to" / "symphony of" family

**Signal:** abstract nouns doing work that should be done by concrete verbs + specific images.

**Fix:** replace the abstraction with a specific action or sensory image grounded in the POV character.

**Before (AI-flagged):**
> The inn stood as a testament to the long tradition of crossroads keepers.

**After (human):**
> The sign squeaked the same three notes every time the wind pushed it — complaint, complaint, complaint. Long-dead keepers had heard that same squeak. Briar could taste it in her teeth.

## Pattern 2 — Tricolon stacking

**Signal:** three-part parallel constructions back-to-back. One tricolon per scene is fine; back-to-back reads as AI-generated rhythm.

**Fix:** break one tricolon into a fragment or a different rhythm. Keep at most one per paragraph.

**Before:**
> She counted the cups. She stacked the plates. She folded the apron. The fire was warm, the light was low, the hour was late.

**After:**
> She counted the cups. Stacked the plates. The apron she folded because folding an apron was a thing her hands knew how to do without asking permission. The fire had settled. The hour had not.

## Pattern 3 — Em-dash overuse

**Signal:** em-dashes used three-plus times in a single passage, often as a prose-rhythm smoothing crutch.

**Fix:** replace some with periods, parentheses, or just straight sentences. Dashes should punctuate a real interruption, not a prose flourish.

## Pattern 4 — "Not just X but Y" construction

**Signal:** the canonical AI rhetorical inversion. Reads as instantly-generated.

**Fix:** replace with a direct statement or a specific observation.

**Before:**
> The cat was not just sitting on the deed, but asserting that the deed was now part of its geography.

**After:**
> The cat sat on the deed. The deed was now under a cat. That was a thing that had happened.

## Pattern 5 — Smooth transitions

**Signal:** passage flows from beat to beat without friction. Every sentence lands exactly where the previous one pointed.

**Fix:** introduce a small interruption — a fragment, a digression in POV character's interior voice, an intrusive sensory detail that doesn't serve the plot but belongs.

## Pattern 6 — Abstract emotion naming

**Signal:** "She felt grief." / "He was angry." / "The moment was poignant."

**Fix:** show the emotion through body, action, or the character's unwillingness to name it.

**Before:**
> She felt unsettled by the flickering hearth.

**After:**
> Her hand on the kettle had stopped halfway. She set the kettle down. Picked it up. Set it down again. Looked at the hearth. Did not look away.

## Pattern 7 — Generic-fantasy vocabulary

**Signal:** "mystical," "ancient," "mysterious," "ethereal," "enigmatic." Detector-triggers because they're statistically associated with AI-generated fantasy.

**Fix:** replace with specific, earned sensory detail. If a character would notice something as "mysterious," let the reader earn that conclusion themselves.

## Pattern 8 — Paragraph length uniformity

**Signal:** consecutive paragraphs of similar length (e.g., all 3-5 sentences). Human prose varies — fragments, 1-sentence paragraphs, long rolling paragraphs mixed.

**Fix:** break up the rhythm. One-sentence paragraph for emphasis. Fragment. Then a longer observation.

## Pattern 9 — Sentence-opener uniformity

**Signal:** most sentences start subject-first ("She did X. She thought Y."). Human prose varies openers with participials, dependent clauses, dialogue, imperatives.

**Fix:** rewrite two or three sentences per paragraph to start with something other than subject-verb.

## Pattern 10 — Adverb-heavy description

**Signal:** "walked slowly," "spoke quietly," "smiled warmly." Adverbs covering for imprecise verbs.

**Fix:** replace verb + adverb with a more specific verb.

**Before:**
> She walked slowly toward the fire.

**After:**
> She drifted toward the fire. (Or, better, a specific gesture: she took the long way around the table because the table was between her and the fire and she was not in a hurry.)

## Do NOT

- Do NOT add typos or grammatical errors to "humanize" text. Pangram doesn't reward errors; it penalizes smoothness.
- Do NOT insert random em-dashes or fragments to trick the detector. Craft, not cosmetics.
- Do NOT restructure plot or character to chase score. If score flags structure-level issues, that's a dev-edit problem, not humanization.
- Do NOT delete flagged passages wholesale. Rewrite them; don't excise them.

If flagged content is essential plot + can't be rewritten humanly, escalate to Board — the detector may be catching a genuinely-generic passage that needs Eleanor-level intervention.
