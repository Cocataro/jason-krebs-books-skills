# CMOS 18 Quick Anchors

Fiction-relevant reference. Released Sept 2024 — current. Remember: **humanizing-fiction directive overrides CMOS where they conflict.**

## Punctuation

### Serial comma (Oxford comma)
- CMOS 6.19: **YES** — use the serial comma.
- House rule: match CMOS. Non-negotiable.

### Em-dash (—)
- CMOS 6.85: **closed** (no spaces around it): word—word
- Humanizing-fiction constraint: limit to <1 parenthetical em-dash per 2 paragraphs. Overuse is an AI-fingerprint.

### En-dash (–)
- Ranges (pp. 12–15)
- Compound modifiers where one part is multiword (Chicago–area, post–World War II)
- Score lines

### Ellipsis
- CMOS 13.50: three spaced dots with a space before and after: `word . . . word`
- Sentence-end: four dots (period + ellipsis): `word. . . .`
- Typographic ellipsis character (…) acceptable if consistent

### Quotation marks
- CMOS 6.9: periods and commas INSIDE closing quote: `"word,"` not `"word",`
- Colons and semicolons OUTSIDE: `"word";`
- Question marks: depends on whether the question is part of the quotation

### Internal thought
- CMOS 13.43: italics for direct interior thought (no quotes)
- House rule: match CMOS unless style sheet specifies otherwise per character/book

## Numbers

- CMOS 9.2: **spell out** whole numbers 1–100 (and larger numbers when they're round)
- Exceptions: precise measurements, addresses, dates
- House rule in fiction: often use CMOS's alternative rule — spell out 1–9, numerals for 10+. Pick one per series, lock in style sheet.

## Italics

### For emphasis
- CMOS 7.50: use sparingly — loses impact when overused

### Foreign words
- CMOS 7.53: italicize on first use; roman thereafter
- Exception: words in common English usage (croissant, piñata) — roman

### Invented words in fiction
- CMOS doesn't explicitly cover — house rule: italicize first use, roman thereafter. Heartland Fantasy under-italicizes for readability.

### Thoughts
- See Internal thought above

### Ship / aircraft names
- Italicize (rarely relevant in Heartland Fantasy)

### Book titles, film titles
- Italicize in running text

## Dialogue

### Tags and punctuation
- CMOS 13.39: comma before dialogue tag (inside quote)
- "Hello," she said. (not "Hello." she said. and not "Hello", she said.)
- Dialogue tag with action: "Hello." She smiled. (action is separate sentence)

### Attribution
- "said" is invisible — use it almost exclusively
- Adverb-modified tags ("-ly") weaken: "he said softly" → consider action beat instead
- Exotic tags (exclaimed, proclaimed, ejaculated) — avoid

## Dashes in dialogue

- Interrupted speech: em-dash ending (or start)
- Trailing off: ellipsis

## Paragraph breaks in dialogue

- New speaker = new paragraph. Always.
- Same speaker across multiple paragraphs: open quote at each paragraph, close only at the last.

## Capitalization

- Titles: headline-style (CMOS 8.159) for headings in some contexts; sentence-case for chapter titles is acceptable per house choice
- In-world proper nouns (places, gods, offices): capitalize
- Magic system concepts: house choice (Le Guin lowercase vs. Rowling uppercase) — pick, lock in style sheet

## When to deviate from CMOS

Whenever the Series Style Guide explicitly says so. Examples:
- Fragments for rhythm / humanizing-fiction → preserve
- Run-ons during emotional beats → preserve
- Comma splices as deliberate voice → preserve
- Unconventional paragraph breaks → preserve

Codify every deviation in the style sheet under §7 CMOS Exceptions.
